AI Use: Pre-Med vs Art Student

Overview

This interview compares how two students in very different fields use artificial intelligence in their academic work. Malia is a pre-med student whose coursework focuses on memorization, accuracy, and responsibility, while Brynn is an art student whose projects emphasize creativity, experimentation, and personal expression. Although they use the same AI tools, their goals, trust levels, and concerns are very different. Their disciplines shape how AI fits into their thinking and workflow.

Pre-Med Student: Malia

What Malia uses AI for

Malia uses AI mainly to make studying faster and more organized. Because her classes involve large amounts of reading and memorization, she relies on AI to condense information into manageable pieces.

  • Summarizing long lecture notes and textbook chapters
  • Creating flashcards and practice quizzes
  • Explaining complicated biological processes in simpler terms
  • Outlining essays before she rewrites everything herself
Trust + verification

Malia does not fully trust AI outputs. Because medicine requires precision, she double-checks everything with her textbooks, slides, and professors’ notes. She described AI as helpful but “confident even when wrong,” so she treats it as a starting point rather than an authority.

Ethics concerns

She worries that over-relying on AI could weaken her understanding and make her dependent. In a field where people’s health is involved, she feels it is important to truly know the material herself. For that reason, she tries solving problems on her own first before asking AI for help.

Art Student: Brynn

What Brynn uses AI for

Brynn uses AI less like a study tool and more like a creative partner. Instead of looking for correct answers, she uses it to spark ideas and push her thinking in new directions.

  • Brainstorming project themes and concepts
  • Generating unusual prompts or poetic descriptions
  • Helping draft or refine artist statements
  • Creating references that she then edits with her own style
Trust + evaluation

Brynn is less concerned about factual accuracy. In art, unexpected or “wrong” results can actually be interesting. She judges AI outputs based on whether they feel meaningful or inspiring rather than whether they are technically correct.

Ethics concerns

Her biggest concern is originality. She worries that if everyone uses the same AI prompts, artwork might start to look similar or generic. She wants to protect her personal voice and avoid letting AI replace her own creative thinking.

Conclusion

These interviews show that AI is not used the same way in every discipline. In science-based fields like pre-med, it functions mainly as a productivity tool that must be checked carefully for accuracy. In creative fields like art, it becomes a generator of ideas and experimentation. Ultimately, how students use AI reflects what their field values most. Precision for medicine and originality for art.
