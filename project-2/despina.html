Despina and the divided approach

This website is built around the central idea of Despina: that the same city appears differently depending on the direction from which it is approached. Despina is not changed by its architecture alone. It is changed by longing, expectation, and distance. What one traveler sees as arrival, another sees as escape.

On the landing screen, the city is split into two fields. One belongs to the sea, and the other to the desert. The division is not meant to show two different cities, but two different emotional readings of the same one. The blue side imagines harbor, rest, and solidity after open water. The desert side imagines release, motion, and the sea still to come after dryness and exhaustion.

The sound reinforces this idea. Ocean and desert audio do not just decorate the page; they act as atmospheres of expectation. Before the city is even entered, it is already being interpreted. The user does not encounter Despina neutrally. They approach it through desire.

Once inside, the city remains the same in structure, but its atmosphere shifts depending on which perspective is active. Reflection, haze, color, and sound all frame the same urban form differently. This mirrors Calvino’s idea that cities are never only what they are materially. They are also what they promise to the person approaching them.

In Invisible Cities, Despina is described from opposite viewpoints: to the camel driver arriving from the desert, it suggests ships, departure, and the sea; to the sailor arriving from the sea, it suggests camels, inland caravans, and the desert. Each sees in the city the world they do not currently inhabit. Despina therefore becomes a city of projection, where longing determines perception.

This website translates that idea by making approach the main interaction. Rather than presenting Despina as a fixed object, it presents it as a city continuously redefined by where the viewer stands, what they lack, and what they imagine lies beyond it.
