Decoded network

Ersilia after the web

At first, these fragments stack like buildings. They suggest towers, blocks, rooms, and urban silhouettes. But after the city has had time to settle, the architecture loosens and the larger structure becomes visible: every fragment belongs to something beyond itself. The site then shifts so that the full network can be read at once.

This is the main idea of the website. Rather than drawing Ersilia as a traditional city of streets, facades, and monuments, the page treats architecture as something assembled from relationships. Each box is a small relational fragment. When these fragments gather, they momentarily resemble buildings. When the connections intensify, the buildings become less important than the threads between them. The city stops being an object and becomes a system.

The words inside the boxes are not quotations from the novel. They are written as traces of kinship, trade, memory, power, desire, and obligation. Each fragment suggests that what holds a city together may be social, emotional, economic, or remembered before it is ever formal.

In Calvino’s Ersilia, the inhabitants stretch strings from the corners of houses to show the relationships that structure their lives. As the network thickens, it becomes so dense that the city can no longer be clearly lived in or even understood. The people leave and construct another Ersilia elsewhere, while the abandoned site remains as a suspended web of relations.

So the city is both present and absent: made of houses, yet more truly made of the invisible ties between them. What remains is a relational ruin.

This website translates that idea by letting you first read the fragments as architecture and then, after a delay, read them as a network that outlasts them.
