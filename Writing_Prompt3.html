For me it’s different because I’m in architecture, so this isn’t just some abstract idea about design, it’s literally what I’m going to be doing every day. I think about it a lot in the studio, where it’s not enough for something to just “work.” Like yeah, it needs to function, but if that’s all it does, it kind of falls flat. I’ve noticed that the projects I actually care about are the ones where there’s something more going on like a feeling, a mood, or even just a clear intention behind why something looks or feels the way it does. When I’m designing, I’m not just thinking about layout or structure, I’m thinking about how someone moves through it, what they notice, what they remember after they leave. That part feels just as important as anything technical. And since this is my career, I don’t want to end up making things that feel generic or could exist anywhere. I want my work to actually respond to where it is and who it’s for. Even if it’s subtle, I think people can tell when a space was designed with care versus when it was just put together to meet requirements. That difference is what makes architecture feel meaningful to me. I think I actually lean into constraints more than I used to, especially in studio. At first it feels frustrating like why limit yourself when you could just make something easier or cleaner, but I’ve noticed that the moments where I’m forced to work within something tight are usually when the project gets more interesting. It makes you slow down and actually think instead of defaulting to the most obvious solution. Same with choosing a harder or less convenient route. Like spending way too long on a physical model instead of just rendering something, or reworking a drawing by hand instead of fixing it instantly on the computer. It’s not efficient at all, but you end up understanding the project differently. You catch things you wouldn’t notice otherwise, and it starts to feel more intentional instead of just produced. I think the slower, more effortful approach forces a kind of presence. You’re actually in the process instead of just trying to get to the end. And that changes the outcome; it usually feels more thoughtful, more specific, and honestly more personal. So in terms of what this says about people, I don’t think we always want things to be as easy as possible. Convenience is great, but if everything is instant and frictionless, it kind of removes the chance to engage deeply. There’s less room for discovery, or even for making something your own.I think the best kinds of tools or technologies aren’t the ones that erase all effort, but the ones that support it in the right way. Like they make things possible without taking away the thinking, the exploration, or the feeling of actually making something.
